  1. User Story 1: As a user, I want Athena to assist me in generating schedules so that the time taken to create a schedule is cut down.
  1. In our problem of study, we stated that scheduling in Athena is confusing and inefficient because it takes a long time to create one’s schedule. From this problem, we generated this specific user story with the aim of cutting down on scheduling making time. Our prototype addresses this by designing a way to automatically generate schedules for the user. Specifically, our prototype includes the design for a system where the user can enter in their classes, block off unavailable times, and get schedule options generated for them. This process will significantly cut down on time taken to create a schedule because the user no longer has to pick the correct classes themselves and make sure that class times work out with each other.

 

  1. User Story 2: As a user, I want the option to select available classes as I type in a class name so that I can easily multi-select classes through their names.
  1. Another issue we found in our problem of study is that Athena’s class sign up process is difficult to navigate around because nothing appears until you press enter. This is why we came up with an user story with the aim of having an easier class sign up process, specifically through multi-select.

Athena sign up design:

In our prototype, we redesigned the class sign up process by using a dropdown with checkboxes that appears as the user types class names in order to fulfill the user story’s need to multi-select classes as they are typing in class names.

Additionally, to further adjust to the user’s desire to select available classes, our prototype has a dropdown with radios for selecting different class times and seeing how they look on a calendar. This design not only allows for the ability to select different available classes but also different available class times.

 

  1. As a user, I want the windows on the registration page to display the most relevant information more prominently so that I can register for my classes easier.
  1. In our problem of study, we also found that important information such as prerequisites and class capacity were unable to be easily found. Therefore, we came up with the user story that we wanted more prominent ways to display relevant information. In order to fulfill this, we designed pop ups that appear on hover or on registration, so the urgent and important information is easily viewable.

Part B:

Our research question is “Do students prefer to register for classes on our revamped versions of Athena better than the current version?” Based on this research question, we decided to use a blend of an experiment and a survey for our methodology. Because our research question is based on user experience rather than a tangible outcome, just an experiment would not be sufficient in addressing our question. Also, if we just sent out a survey with video demonstrations instead of having the students interact with the website or prototype, the results may not be accurate to how the students would feel if they had interactive capabilities. According to an article on user-experience research methods by Christian Rohrer, when assessing a product, or measuring “product performance against itself or its competition,” surveys and A/B testing are the best approaches to the research (1). A/B testing is a procedure for testing designs based on randomly assigned groups of users each interacting with different designs and “measuring the effect of these assignments on user behavior” (1).  For these reasons, we will be conducting an experiment where the goal of the subject is to register for a certain schedule of classes, and a survey will be administered afterwards with questions regarding how the user felt about their experience.

For our testing procedure, the first step is to find participants for the study. We are limiting the scope of participants to students at the University of Georgia. This is because, according to A Human Activity Approach to User Interfaces, it is important for the user interface to not be seen independently of the use activity (2). This means that a user interface is best used by those who will actually be using it in real life, so students who have used Athena would be best suited for the study. We could send out a short survey to gather interest on the UGA listserv, similar to other research studies at the university. Once we have a sufficient number of people interested in the study, we could set up a Zoom call to conduct the study while adhering to pandemic safety regulations. Each of the subjects will be randomly assigned one of four groups: the control group, prototype 1 (P1), prototype 2 (P)2, and prototype 3 (P3). The three prototype groups will conduct the experiment using the prototypes from the first, second, and third user stories respectively. The control group will use a prototype of the Athena registration page as it is. Before the experiment, a consent form will be sent to each of the participants while on the Zoom call. The form will detail how the data will be anonymous, how no personal information will be collected from the participant, how the study is completely voluntary and the participant can stop at any time, and any other concerns that the participant may have. The test proctor will be there to answer any questions the participant may have about the consent form.

Once the consent form has been given, the proctor will then send a link to a webpage to the subject based on the group that the participant has been assigned. On the home page, there will be a video demonstration on how to register for a class in the specific prototype. A video demonstration is important to include because it is a much more interactive way of displaying information that the participant needs to successfully complete the study. We will use specific guidelines that will help the video be understandable and useful to the user. (4)  The next page will be a description of the task the participant is required to do. This task will be registering for a given schedule of three classes. The only requirement for this task is that the schedule must be valid (i.e. the classes must not overlap, the course sections must not be full, and the prerequisites for the class must be completed). Throughout this process and before the test begins, the participants will have the opportunity to ask the proctor any questions they have about the information they received. Once the participant is ready to start the experiment, the user clicks the start button and the beginning web page for each prototype is given to the user.

Once the user is done registering for their classes successfully, they can hit a “Finish” button and the test will be concluded. The proctor will then send a follow-up survey to the participant for them to complete. The questions for this survey are outlined below. All of the survey responses are quantitative, and they will all be analyzed as such. Each participant's responses will be anonymous. According to Christian Rohrer, the best way to approach responses that assess a product in relation to competition is “mainly quantitative” research  (1). This is why the survey questions are predominantly quantitative. The data will be sent to a Google Sheets page automatically so that the data can be viewed easily and can be compared and analyzed easily. As shown in the questions below, the analysis of the survey will help answer our research question by showing the average user experience results for each of the prototypes in relation to the original Athena. If one or more of the prototypes are preferred to Athena’s current registration page, then the answer to our question would be yes.

Survey Questions

Overall, how did you feel about using this type of class registration?

(Strongly Dissatisfied, Dissatisfied, Slightly Dissatisfied, Neutral, Slightly Satisfied, Satisfied, Strongly Satisfied)

On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate the ease of moving between different screens?

On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate the clarity of prompts while using the website?

On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate the availability of information presented?

How would you rate the time it took to generate a schedule?

(Faster than Average, Average, Slower than Average)

How helpful did you find the provided demo video?

(Very Unhelpful, Unhelpful, Slightly Unhelpful, Neutral, Slightly Helpful, Helpful, Very Helpful)

If you had any questions for the proctor, how helpful were they in answering your questions?

(No Questions, Very Unhelpful, Unhelpful, Slightly Unhelpful, Neutral, Slightly Helpful, Helpful, Very Helpful)

How would you feel about using this software in the future?

(Strongly Dissatisfied, Dissatisfied, Slightly Dissatisfied, Neutral, Slightly Satisfied, Satisfied, Strongly Satisfied)
